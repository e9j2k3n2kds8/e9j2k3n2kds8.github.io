RECOMMENDATIONS

By Joe Orange

Home Books FAQ Contact

These are not the ones everyone mentions. You won't find the same fifteen titles that appear on every "best of" list. This is a list for people who watch a lot — the ones who have already worked their way through the obvious and are looking for something they might have missed. Some of these are obscure. Some were buried by bad timing, a limited release, or the simple bad luck of arriving before anyone was paying attention. A few are single episodes from anthology series that most people have never heard of. The list also includes some Twilight Zone episodes — because some of them deserve to be talked about more than they are, and they fit here better than anywhere else. The only rule for making this list is that it stayed with me.

Films

Film 2018

Border

Dir. Ali Abbasi · Swedish

Weird done right. That's the only way to put it. Border is a film that commits completely to its premise and never flinches, which is rarer than it sounds. The makeup is some of the best I have ever seen — not the Hollywood kind designed to be admired, but the kind that makes you genuinely uneasy without quite knowing why. The characters are the same way. There is an undertone running through the whole film that something is deeply wrong, but the film never telegraphs it. It just lets it sit there. I haven't stopped thinking about it. Won the Un Certain Regard award at Cannes and was nominated for an Academy Award for Best Makeup. Most people still haven't seen it.

Film 2009

Gentlemen Broncos

Dir. Jared Hess

A film about a science fiction writer, made by people who clearly understand science fiction writers — which is rarer than it sounds. A young writer attends a fantasy and sci-fi writing camp and has his story stolen by the most celebrated author in the field, a man played by Jemaine Clement in what I would argue is one of the funniest performances of the last twenty years. Clement commits to the role with a completely straight face, which is the only way this kind of comedy works and the reason so many people try it and fail. Hess directed Napoleon Dynamite, and some of that same deadpan energy is here — the absurdity is played completely seriously, and that is where all the laughs come from. The film has a 6.2 on IMDb and I genuinely cannot account for that number. It is much funnier than its reputation suggests, much stranger than its marketing implied, and the kind of thing that stays with you precisely because it takes its ridiculous subject completely seriously. If you have ever written anything, or wanted to, there is something in here that will make you laugh harder than you expect.

TV Episodes

TV Episode 1984

Child's Play

Fox Mystery Theater (Hammer House of Mystery and Suspense)

A small number of people. One location. A problem that makes no sense and keeps getting worse. That is the entire formula, and when it works, nothing beats it. This episode works. Mary Crosby — Bing Crosby's daughter — leads a cast dealing with something that I won't spoil, except to say that it escalates in exactly the right way. It's a masterclass in ordinary people facing an extraordinary problem with no good options. A single episode, easy to find, and one that most people have never come across. Exactly the kind of thing this list exists for.

TV Miniseries 1995

The Langoliers

Stephen King · Adapted from Four Past Midnight

A lot of people talk about liminal spaces these days — that feeling of being in a place that exists between somewhere and somewhere else. An empty airport at 3am. A shopping mall after closing. A corridor that should have people in it but doesn't. The wrongness isn't in what's there. It's in what's missing. The Langoliers is one of the earliest and best expressions of that feeling on screen. Passengers on a near-empty overnight flight wake up to find the world below has gone quiet in a way that is hard to name but impossible to ignore. Based on Stephen King's novella from Four Past Midnight — a book worth reading on its own — this adaptation is not widely seen and deserves to be. The CGI has dated badly, but the atmosphere hasn't.

TV Miniseries 1999

Storm of the Century

Stephen King · Original screenplay

Most Stephen King adaptations begin somewhere else — a novel, a short story, something he wrote for the page first. Storm of the Century is different. King wrote it directly as a television screenplay, which means every scene, every line, every beat of it was designed for the screen from the start. There is no source novel to compare it to and no room for a producer to argue that the book was better. This is the thing itself. A massive blizzard descends on Little Tall Island, a small Maine community cut off from the mainland, and a stranger walks into town who seems to know everything about everyone — every secret, every sin. He wants something specific. His terms are simple. The Pied Piper of Hamelin is an old story, and King finds the modern version of it here, buried under snow, in a town full of people who thought their worst secrets were their own. Outside of serious King fans, almost no one I have spoken to has seen this. It deserves a much wider audience.

TV Miniseries 1983

V

Dir. Kenneth Johnson · NBC

There was a period in the early 1980s when the answer to almost any problem on television was a small group of determined people with a plan, limited resources, and more nerve than sense. V is the purest version of that formula I know. Alien visitors arrive. They look friendly. They aren't. What follows is essentially a story about occupation and resistance — the parallels to wartime Europe are not subtle, and they are not meant to be. The show was made with a specific point in mind about how ordinary people respond when authority turns hostile, and it makes that point clearly and without flinching. A ragtag group of civilians, a handful of weapons, and the conviction that it is worth fighting back. If you haven't seen it, it holds up better than you might expect. If you saw it as a kid and remember it fondly, it holds up better than that too. Worth revisiting.

TV Series 2004

Garth Marenghi's Darkplace

Channel 4 · Created by Matthew Holness & Richard Ayoade

Six episodes. That is all there is, and that is the great tragedy of it. The premise is this: Garth Marenghi is a horror author — prolific, self-important, completely convinced of his own genius — who in the 1980s wrote, directed, and starred in his own television production. That show was apparently too frightening to broadcast at the time and is only now being screened. What you are watching, in other words, is a deliberate recreation of a bad 1980s hospital horror drama, made by a man with no self-awareness and a very high opinion of his own work. The humour is completely meta and completely committed. The acting is purposely wooden. The production values are purposely wrong. The voiceover narration is purposely awful. And yet the jokes land, again and again, because the show understands exactly what it is sending up. Matthew Holness plays Marenghi with total conviction, which is the only way this kind of comedy works. If you write — especially if you write genre fiction and have ever encountered the particular brand of self-seriousness that sometimes comes with it — there is something in Darkplace that will feel uncomfortably familiar. Most people have never seen it. Most people who have seen it think about it for years afterwards.
